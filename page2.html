What are the main changes from sixth form to computational thinking at university?

In the transition from A-Level Computer Science to the foundation year, several key differences can be observed. The foundation course places a stronger emphasis on computational thinking, focusing on how problems are structured and solved in a more formalized way.

Unlike A-Levels, where programming and theoretical knowledge are assessed separately, this course integrates both aspects, requiring students to apply problem-solving techniques in practical scenarios.

Additionally, the foundation course introduces a greater emphasis on group work and collaboration. While A-Level projects were often individual efforts, the foundation course involves workshops and practical assignments where teamwork is a key component. This helps students develop communication and collaboration skills that are essential in the industry.

One of the key differences lies in more group and collabarative involvement. For most people in their previous education, any computer science project was individual and marked individually. In this unit, is is highly likely you that you with groups of people you may not know. The coursework in the unit requires to work in these groups and arrange your own meetings.

Below are some of the topics which are in the Computer Science A-Level

  1. Algorithms and data structures - Using a programming language
  2. Theory of Computation
  3. Binary number system
  4. Hardware, software and computer systems
  5. Individual programming project of your choice

Computer science at sixth form involved both theoretical and practical aspects of computing

Below are some of the topics which are covered in the Foundation Year Computational Thinking Module.

  1. Algorithms - Understanding algorithms
  2. Ways of representing algorithms - Flowcharts and Pseudocode
  3. Graphs - Nodes and edges, traversals
  4. Abstraction and Generalisation
  5. Algorithm complexity - Analysing algorithms
  6. Recursive algorithms

Computational thinking focuses on understanding algorithms rather than actually learning a specific programming language.

Some of the changes are shown in the table below:

Section Weight Foundation Year
Problem Solving Focuses on structured algorithms and step-by-step problem-solving. Introduces decomposition, abstraction, and generalization to solve complex problems efficiently.
Recursion Basic introduction with simple examples like factorial and Fibonacci sequences. Gives a deeper understanding of what recursion is used for.
Computational Complexity Only briefly touched upon in some syllabus areas. Introduces formal and informal complexity analysis to evaluate algorithm efficiency.
Collaboration & Group Work Mostly individual assignments and programming tasks. Group-based problem-solving workshops and discussions are an integral part of learning.
Practical Assessments Coursework projects focus on implementing algorithms and software. Includes hands-on practical skills assessments, where students contribute to group projects and discussions.
